\title{Landauer Erasure and the Quantum of Action}

\begin{abstract}
Quantum microstates are operationally indistinguishable inside Planck's quantum of action. We show that among these infinite possible microstates, Landauer's principle compels the selection of the microstate requiring the least action to erase. Thermodynamic necessity granulates the continuum. It resolves the coarse-grained configurations of quantum mechanics at low action into the fine-grained configurations of classical mechanics at high action.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

While the uncertainty principle is typically framed as a statistical limit \cite{Robertson1929}, our starting point is its geometric foundation: Planck's quantum of action, $h$ \cite{Planck1901, Planck1906}. Imagine this unit not as a statistical blur, but as a de Gosson blob in phase space \cite{DeGosson2008}. 

Without loss of generality, we consider a 1D system. The environment can stretch and squeeze this blob into various shapes, but its area $A_b=h/2$ is conserved \cite{Gromov1985}. We will see later that Zurek's derivation of sub-Planck structure \cite{Zurek2001} necessitates a conjugate pair of $h/2$ blobs whose geometric sum is the quantum of action $h$, but visualizing a single blob provides a helpful starting point.

Because no physical measurement can resolve the interior of the quantum of action, the infinite possible microstates within it remain operationally indistinguishable \cite{Brillouin1956, Peres1993, VonNeumann1932, Gromov1985}. However, specifying one of these microstates requires physical information. According to Landauer's principle \cite{Landauer1961}, a bit of information must be instantiated in a physical degree of freedom, and erasing it requires a physical expenditure of action. This manifests as transferring phase-space volume from the system to the thermal bath: a thermodynamic cost of heat $k_B T \ln{2}$ dissipated over a finite amount of time $\tau$ for each bit.

Physical systems are strictly bounded by their kinematic capacity---a finite amount of available classical action $A = \pi \Delta q \Delta p \ge h/2$. The system cannot expend more action on the thermal bath than its classical boundaries contain, $A_L \le A$ \cite{Lloyd2000}. Consequently, the universe does not possess the kinematic budget to fund infinite phase-space precision; to pinpoint a continuum value with an infinitely long sequence of information would require an infinite, impossible amount of Landauer action, $A_L$.

Faced with this finite action budget, the universe must truncate. It leverages the many-to-one mapping of indistinguishable microstates $\{(q_\mu, p_\mu)\}$ sharing the same physical identity. Since any coordinate in this pool is physically valid, the universe selects the specific microstate $(q_\mu^*, p_\mu^*)$ that requires the least Landauer action to erase, $A_L^*$ \cite{FeynmanHibbs1965, Landauer1961}. Clearing these minimal bits to return from this state to the ground state is an irreversible thermodynamic process perfectly bounded by the system's classical action limit $A_L^* \le A$.

To illustrate this physical truncation, consider the problem of pinpointing a coordinate in the continuum. The universe operates as a discrete Szilard engine \cite{Szilard1929}, narrowing down a location by repeatedly inserting partitions to make a sequence of binary choices \cite{PlenioVitelli2001}. Suppose the center of a quantum blob, $q_b$, sits at an irrational coordinate relative to a classical reference scale $q_0$, such that their ratio $q_b/q_0 = \phi \approx 0.618 \dots$ is the little golden ratio. While a machine that understands symbolic algebra could express this as a finite sequence $\phi = (\sqrt{5}-1)/2$, a physical Szilard engine would require an infinite sequence of binary choices (e.g., $\phi = \mathtt{1010\overline{10}}, N=\infty$) to resolve it. In contrast, a rational coordinate like $+1/2$ terminates after a finite number, $N$, of partitions, requiring a short binary sequence (e.g., $\mathtt{10}, N=2$, see Fig.~\ref{fig:erasure_tree}). Bounded by available classical action, the universe halts the Szilard engine, abandoning the infinitely long sequence of the little golden ratio. It settles instead for a nearby coordinate with a short binary encoding—a substitution permitted because both coordinates remain operationally indistinguishable inside the quantum blob.

In this letter, we show how Landauer erasure inside Planck's quantum of action leads to quantum mechanics and satisfies the correspondence principle. The physical necessity to minimize this Landauer action dictates the resolution of phase space, driving the transition from discrete quantum microstates at low action to continuous classical trajectories at high action.

\section{The Quantum of Action}

Given phase-space coordinates $(q,p)$, where $q$ is position and $p$ is momentum, the Heisenberg uncertainty principle is $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$ \cite{Heisenberg1927}. Geometrically, symplectic mechanics translates this statistical variance into a $1\sigma$ covariance ellipse in phase space, bounding the minimum uncertainty region of the state as $q^2/(2\sigma_q^2) + p^2/(2\sigma_p^2) \le 1$ \cite{DeGosson2006}. The geometric area $A$ of this ellipse is given by $A = \pi (\sqrt{2}\sigma_q) (\sqrt{2}\sigma_p) = 2\pi \sigma_q \sigma_p$.

De Gosson bridges this statistical uncertainty with phase-space geometry. By saturating the Heisenberg bound ($\sigma_q \sigma_p = \hbar/2$), the minimal permissible area emerges as the quantum blob $A_b$ \cite{DeGosson2003}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SimpleLanguageOneBlob}
\frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{Quantum of Action} = \text{Quantum Blob}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SymplecticCapacity}
\frac{h}{2} = A_b
\end{equation}

Protected by Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{Gromov1985}, this $h/2$ minimum area is topologically conserved. Compressing the blob along one axis forces a proportional expansion along its conjugate.

Zurek \cite{Zurek2001} demonstrated that a quantum state develops localized structures as the classical action boundaries, $\Delta p$ and $\Delta q$, compress the conjugate squeezed boundaries, $\delta q = \hbar / \Delta p$ and $\delta p = \hbar / \Delta q$. Expressing these squeezed boundaries as phase-space ellipses yields a conjugate pair of de Gosson blobs (see Fig.~\ref{fig:erase_schematic}) with areas $\pi \delta q \Delta p = h/2$ and $\pi \Delta q \delta p = h/2$.

Because both areas are required to bound the space, the total quantum of action $h$ is defined by their sum:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SimpleLanguageBlobs}
\text{Quantum of Action} = \text{Pair of Quantum Blobs}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Blobs}
h = \pi \delta q \Delta p + \pi \Delta q \delta p
\end{equation}

Furthermore, Zurek showed that quantum interference of the compass state creates structures bounded by these squeezed boundaries, with rectangular area $a = \delta q \delta p$ \cite{Zurek2001}. Generalizing to symplectic geometry, the unique maximum-volume shape inscribed within this convex body is the Fritz John ellipsoid \cite{John1948}, shown as the interior uncertainty ellipse in Fig.~\ref{fig:erase_schematic}. While the overall state cannot be compressed below de Gosson's $h/2$ limit \cite{DeGosson2022}, the resolving power required to erase a microstate within these squeezed boundaries forces this inner geometry to be strictly smaller:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:JohnEllipsoid}
\pi \delta q \delta p \le \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

This establishes a classical action boundary on squeezed uncertainty:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:GeometricUncertaintyPrinciplePlainLanguage}
\text{Squeezed Uncertainty} \propto \frac{1}{\text{Classical Action}}
\end{equation}

Substituting the squeezed boundaries, the area of this inscribed ellipse is $a_b = \pi \delta q \delta p = \pi(\hbar/\Delta p)(\hbar/\Delta q)$, yielding the squeezed uncertainty equation:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SqueezedUncertaintyPrinciple}
\delta q \delta p = \frac{\hbar^2}{\Delta q \Delta p}.
\end{equation}

While the traditional uncertainty principle $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$ demands the macroscopic quantum states underlying Schr{\"{o}}dinger's cat paradox \cite{Schrodinger1980Trimmer}, this squeezed uncertainty principle precludes those states and the cat paradox: as classical boundaries expand, they compress the conjugate blobs into fine crosshairs, enforcing the correspondence principle (see the bottom panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:harmonic_oscillator}).

\section{Landauer Erasure}

Landauer's principle establishes the minimum thermodynamic cost of the information erasure of one bit as $E_L = k_B T \ln{2}$ \cite{Landauer1961}.

To express the Landauer erasure of $N$ bits as action, 

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureActionPlain}
\text{Landauer Action} = \text{Bits} \times \text{Energy} \times \text{Time}
\end{equation}

it is dimensionally necessary to introduce a characteristic erasure time, $\tau$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureActionDefinition}
A_L = N E_L \tau
\end{equation}

We validate this definition at the limit of the minimal quantum phase space, where the pair of quantum blobs degenerate into an overlapping circle with area $h/2$. Saturated by one position bit and one momentum bit, $N = N_q + N_p = 2$, the minimal erasure action becomes:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:MinimalErasureAction}
2 E_L \tau = \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

Solving for $\tau$ recovers the Margolus-Levitin limit \cite{MargolusLevitin1998} for the minimum time to evolve to an orthogonal state ($\tau_\perp$):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:MLErasureTime}
\tau_{N=2} = \frac{h}{4 E_L} = \tau_\perp
\end{equation}

This demonstrates that the Margolus-Levitin minimal orthogonal time corresponds to the maximal erasure time for a single bit-pair.

Lloyd demonstrated that the number of elementary logical operations $N$ a physical system can perform in time $t$ is bounded by its available energy: $N \le \frac{2 E t}{\pi \hbar}$ \cite{Lloyd2000}. By identifying the erasure of coordinate information as the logical operation of the system, we can map the bits of coordinate precision directly to this computational limit.

Applying this limit to the geometry of phase space, we posit that the total action required to erase $N$ bits of information cannot exceed the classical action area $A$ available to the system:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureAndClassicalActionPlain}
\text{Erasure Action} \le \text{Classical Action}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureAndClassicalAction}
N E_L \tau \le A
\end{equation}

Planck demonstrated \cite{Planck1906} that a classical phase space of area $A$ has a configuration capacity of $A/h$. Expanding the quantum of action $h$ into its pair of quantum blobs (Eq.~\ref{eq:Blobs}), we scale the erasure action (Eq.~\ref{eq:ErasureAndClassicalAction}):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:GeometricActionLimit}
(N_q + N_p) E_L \tau \le \frac{A}{h} (\pi \delta q \Delta p + \pi \Delta q \delta p)
\end{equation}

Where the erasure paths from the selected microstate $(q_\mu^*, p_\mu^*)$ to the blob centers $(q_b, p_b)$ are bounded by $|\epsilon_q^*| \le \delta q$ and $|\epsilon_p^*| \le \delta p$.

\subsection{Anatomy of the Erasure Action}

To understand the physical machinery of Equation~\ref{eq:GeometricActionLimit}, we can isolate the provisioning and expenditure of coordinate bits:

\subsubsection{The Quantum Capacity ($A/h$)}
The area of the conjugate blob pair is the quantum of action, $h$. Because each quantum blob $A_b = h/2$ can only ever fund the minimal bit budget ($N_q = 1, N_p = 1$), the action required to support a classical bit budget ($N \gg 1$) must be provisioned by the classical scale of the system. The multiplier $A/h$ provides this capacity, scaling the quantum of action $h$ up to the classical action necessary to support the total bits in $N$.

\subsubsection{The Geometric Squeezing ($h/2$)}
When a classical boundary expands (e.g., increasing the classical momentum boundary $\Delta p$), symplectic geometry conserves the area of each de Gosson blob at $h/2$. Because this area cannot grow, the blob must physically compress the squeezed position boundary (e.g., $\delta q$) to compensate.

\subsubsection{The Resolving Power ($N$)}
This symplectic squeezing acts as the resolving power of phase space. To isolate a microstate within shrinking squeezed boundaries ($\delta q \to 0$, $\delta p \to 0$), the phase space must be divided into a strictly larger number of partitions ($N_q \to \infty, N_p \to \infty$). Therefore, classical boundaries enforce this squeezed resolution, which directly dictates the number of bits required for Landauer erasure:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ResolvingPowerPlain}
\substack{\text{Classical} \\ \text{Boundary}}
\sim
\substack{\text{Resolving} \\ \text{Power}}
\sim
\substack{\text{Bit} \\ \text{Budget}}
\end{equation}

Isolating the conjugate pairs reveals the causal sequence: the expanding classical boundaries drive the inverse scaling of the squeezed boundaries, which dictates the erasure bit budget:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:BitBudgetResolutionCoordinate}
\Delta p \sim \frac{1}{\delta q} \sim N_q 
\quad \text{and} \quad
\Delta q \sim \frac{1}{\delta p} \sim N_p 
\end{equation}

When the classical momentum boundary ($\Delta p$) grows, the squeezed position boundary ($\delta q$) must shrink. The universe must therefore spend more bits ($N_q$) to select and erase the state. This changing bit budget dictates the physics we observe: low action affords only enough bits for discrete quantum configurations, while high action supplies the bit budget required to draw smooth classical trajectories.

\section{Landauer Erasure Density}

Standard phase-space formulations of quantum mechanics traditionally employ the Wigner quasi-probability distribution $W(q,p)$ \cite{Wigner1932}. In the context of information erasure, however, we instead introduce the Landauer erasure density $L(q,p)$ as a sum of discrete Iverson brackets \cite{Knuth1992}—mathematical switches that snap to exactly 1 when a microstate is occupied and 0 otherwise—over the $M$ accessible microstates:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:JointErasure}
L(q,p) = \sum_{\mu=1}^{M} \left[ q = \frac{A}{\pi \hbar} \epsilon_{q,\mu} \right] \left[ p = \frac{A}{\pi \hbar} \epsilon_{p,\mu} \right]
\end{equation}

Because Landauer erasure evaluates to a discrete coordinate grid, the marginal distributions are obtained by summing over the conjugate variables:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Marginals}
N(q) = \sum_{p} L(q,p)
\quad \text{and} \quad
N(p) = \sum_{q} L(q,p)
\end{equation}

For any phase-space coordinate, $x \in \{q, p\}$, an encoding function selects the microstate $x_\mu^*$ inside the squeezed boundary $\delta x$ centered on the blob coordinate $x_b$, yielding a binary sequence $s_x^*$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:EncoderFunction}
(x_\mu^*, s_x^*) = \operatorname{ENCODE}(x_b, \delta x)
\end{equation}

Following Bennett's protocol \cite{Bennett1973}, we invert and reverse the encoded binary path $s_x^*$ to form the uncomputation path $(\bar{s}_x^*)^R$, where both paths have bit count $N_x = |s_x^*| = |(\bar{s}_x^*)^R|$.

Physical erasure of this sequence uncomputes the microstate back to the blob center ($x_b$). This process expends Landauer action ($\Delta A_L = N_x E_L \tau$) to the environment, yielding the phase-space displacement $\epsilon_x^*$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureFunction}
\epsilon_x^* = \operatorname{ERASE}(s_x^*, x_\mu^*, x_b), \quad \text{expends} \quad \Delta A_L = N_x E_L \tau
\end{equation}

As each selected microstate $(q_\mu^*, p_\mu^*)$ uncomputes to its respective center $(q_b, p_b)$, it generates the conjugate erasure displacements $\epsilon_q^*$ and $\epsilon_p^*$. The observed classical coordinates $(q,p)$ are these displacements scaled by the geometric capacity $\frac{A}{\pi \hbar}$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ObservedCoordinates}
(q,p) = \frac{A}{\pi \hbar} (\epsilon_q^*,\epsilon_p^*)
\end{equation}

\section{Time Evolution and Decoherence}

The physical erasure of the selected microstate unfolds over time. As the system clears these coordinate bits, the microstate steps closer to the classical target, physically shrinking the offsets $\epsilon_q^*$ and $\epsilon_p^*$ toward zero. Because the classical coordinates scale directly with these squeezed offsets, they track this stepwise erasure process:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:TimeEvolutionCoordinates}
q(t) = \frac{A}{\pi \hbar} \epsilon_q^*(t) \quad \text{and} \quad p(t) = \frac{A}{\pi \hbar} \epsilon_p^*(t)
\end{equation}

Quantum behavior is visible only while these bits remain. When the bits are fully erased ($\epsilon_q^* = \epsilon_p^* = 0$), the coordinates rest at the classical target $(q_b, p_b)$. We observe this mechanical loss of information as quantum decoherence.

Because physical action is the product of energy and time, we can determine the decoherence time ($\tau_D$) by dividing the total geometric action by the total Landauer energy cost required to erase the $N$ partitions:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:DecoherenceTime}
\tau_D = \frac{A}{(N_q + N_p) k_B T \ln 2}
\end{equation}

This denominator sets the physical clock. A system maintains its quantum behavior if it remains small ($N \to 2$) and cold ($T \to 0$). If the state requires a large bit budget ($N \gg 1$) or operates at a high temperature ($T \gg 0$), the denominator grows rapidly. The erasure time drops to zero, and the system becomes entirely classical.

\section{Finite or Infinite Support}

Standard phase-space representations utilize infinite Gaussian tails, whereas our Landauer erasure density relies on strict operational boundaries. Because thermodynamic erasure costs are overwhelmingly dominated by the localized core volume, this distinction is physically moot. Furthermore, compact support cleanly eliminates the conceptual artifact of assigning non-zero probabilities at classical distances.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Dimensional Scaling and Phase-Space Geometry}

To computationally simulate the irreversible erasure of microstates, we map the physical dimensions of phase space to a normalized, dimensionless coordinate system. We establish characteristic reference scales $q_0$ and $p_0$ such that their phase-space area defines the minimal quantum blob, $A_0 = \pi q_0 p_0 = h/2$.

Any physical boundary or coordinate can therefore be expressed as a dimensionless ratio, denoted by the $\widetilde{\text{tilde}}$, scaled by its characteristic unit: $\Delta q = \widetilde{\Delta q} q_0$, $\Delta p = \widetilde{\Delta p} p_0$, $\delta q = \widetilde{\delta q} q_0$, and $\delta p = \widetilde{\delta p} p_0$.

The foundation of our topological model rests on four de Gosson ellipsoids. By dividing the physical area of these ellipses by the characteristic capacity $A_0 = h/2$, the physical boundaries reduce to a system of dimensionless geometric constraints:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Classical Action Boundary:} $$\widetilde{\Delta q} \widetilde{\Delta p} \ge 1$$    
    \item \textbf{Squeezed Position Ellipse:} $$\widetilde{\delta q} \widetilde{\Delta p} = 1 \implies \widetilde{\delta q} = 1 / \widetilde{\Delta p}$$
    \item \textbf{Squeezed Momentum Ellipse:} $$\widetilde{\Delta q} \widetilde{\delta p} = 1 \implies \widetilde{\delta p} = 1 / \widetilde{\Delta q}$$
    \item \textbf{Interior Squeezed Ellipse:} $$\widetilde{\delta q} \widetilde{\delta p} = 1 / (\widetilde{\Delta q} \widetilde{\Delta p})$$
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Semiclassical Harmonic Oscillator}

For the symmetrical harmonic oscillator, the dimensionless classical boundaries expand uniformly: $\widetilde{\Delta q} = \widetilde{\Delta p}$.

The relative action is $A/A_0 = \widetilde{\Delta q} \widetilde{\Delta p}$. For a given action level $A$, the classical boundaries are:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\Delta q} = \sqrt{\frac{A}{A_0}} \quad \text{and} \quad \widetilde{\Delta p} = \sqrt{\frac{A}{A_0}}
\end{equation}

Substituting these into the constraints of the squeezed conjugate ellipses yields the sub-Planck limits:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\delta q} = \sqrt{\frac{A_0}{A}} \quad \text{and} \quad \widetilde{\delta p} = \sqrt{\frac{A_0}{A}}
\end{equation}

At the ground state ($A = A_0$), the classical and squeezed boundaries converge: $\widetilde{\Delta q} = \widetilde{\Delta p} = \widetilde{\delta q} = \widetilde{\delta p} = 1$. This topological intersection of four de Gosson ellipsoids marks the phase-space boundary where Heisenberg's uncertainty limit meets Zurek's sub-Planck bounds, defining a single quantum blob.

These dimensionless boundaries describe radial phase-space geometry. Because the harmonic oscillator evolves over a closed periodic orbit, projecting this 2D phase space onto a 1D cross-section requires scaling by the time-averaged linear footprint of the orbit. Integrating the absolute displacement over the full angular period yields a geometric projection factor of $2/\pi$. The projected 1D squeezed position boundary is therefore:

\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\delta q}_{\text{orbit}} = \frac{2}{\pi} \sqrt{\frac{A_0}{A}}
\quad \text{and} \quad
\widetilde{\delta p}_{\text{orbit}} = \frac{2}{\pi} \sqrt{\frac{A_0}{A}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Simulation Architecture}

We simulated a total of 990,199,901 individual erasures across 9,901 discrete values of available classical action, sweeping 100,001 independent classical target coordinates ($\tilde{x}_b$) per action level. To ensure uniform data density and avoid quadratic warping at high energies, we performed a linear scan through the classical action space from $A = 1.0 A_0$ to $A = 100.0 A_0$ with a step size of $0.01 A_0$.

To ensure the binary partitioning of the system preserves physical symmetry, the computational engine strictly evaluates the phase space over a normalized reference interval, $\tilde{x} \in [-1.0, 1.0]$. Therefore, the dynamic physical boundaries must be mapped into this relative coordinate space. Dividing the 1D physical squeezed boundary by the expanding classical macroscopic boundary yields the relative algorithmic tolerance required to resolve a single microstate:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\delta x}_{\text{rel}} = \frac{\widetilde{\delta x}}{\widetilde{\Delta x}} = \frac{\frac{2}{\pi}\sqrt{A_0/A}}{\sqrt{A/A_0}} = \frac{2}{\pi} \frac{A_0}{A}.
\end{equation}
 
For each state, the thermodynamic erasure was computed using a custom C++ simulation engine bound to an R-based parallel data pipeline (\texttt{Rcpp} and \texttt{RcppParallel}). To simulate the physical encoding function $\operatorname{ENCODE}$, the binary phase-space partitioning uses a Stern-Brocot tree \cite{ConcreteMath}, leveraging modified sequences \cite{Aiylam2013} to extend the boundaries to $\pm\infty$ and cover the real line $\mathbb{R}$.

Seeded by the mapped target coordinates ($\tilde{x}_b$) and the relative algorithmic tolerance ($\widetilde{\delta x}_{\text{rel}}$), the fundamental interval is dynamically partitioned into a sequence of binary address bits:

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{ENCODE}{$\tilde{x}_b, \widetilde{\delta x}_{\text{rel}}$}
    \State $\text{left} \gets (-1, 0)$
    \State $\text{num} \gets 0, \quad \text{den} \gets 1$
    \State $\tilde{x}_\mu^* \gets \text{num} / \text{den}$
    \State $\text{right} \gets (1, 0)$
    \State $s_x^* \gets \text{``''}$
    \While{$|\tilde{x}_\mu^* - \tilde{x}_b| > \widetilde{\delta x}_{\text{rel}}$}
        \If{$\tilde{x}_\mu^* < \tilde{x}_b$} 
            \State $\text{left} \gets (\text{num}, \text{den})$
            \State $s_x^* \gets s_x^* + \text{``1''}$
        \Else 
            \State $\text{right} \gets (\text{num}, \text{den})$
            \State $s_x^* \gets s_x^* + \text{``0''}$
        \EndIf
        \State $\text{num} \gets \text{left}_{\text{num}} + \text{right}_{\text{num}}$
        \State $\text{den} \gets \text{left}_{\text{den}} + \text{right}_{\text{den}}$
        \State $\tilde{x}_\mu^* \gets \text{num} / \text{den}$
    \EndWhile
    \State \Return $\tilde{x}_\mu^*, s_x^*$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}

The engine enforces a mathematical condition for compact support; the iterative \texttt{while} loop operates under a closed-set limit, halting the algorithm the moment the relative erasure displacement $|\tilde{\epsilon}_x^*|$ satisfies the mapped squeezed boundary $\widetilde{\delta x}_{\text{rel}}$. This prevents artificial inflation of the Landauer action and ensures the phase-space volume is bounded by the classical action capacity. 

Once encoded, the universe must physically erase the state. For example, let $s_x^* = \mathtt{1101}$. Taking the bitwise complement gives $\bar{s}_x^* = \mathtt{0010}$, and reversing it yields the uncomputation path $(\bar{s}_x^*)^R = \mathtt{0100}$. By following $(\bar{s}_x^*)^R$, the encoded sequence $s_x^*$ is irreversibly destroyed. This erasure dissipates the Landauer action and yields the observable displacement:

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{ERASE}{$(\bar{s}_x^*)^R, \tilde{x}_\mu^*, \tilde{x}_b$}
    \State $N_x \gets |(\bar{s}_x^*)^R|$
    \While{$N_x > 0$}
        \State \Call{ShiftLeft}{$(\bar{s}_x^*)^R$} 
        \Statex \Comment{Expends action $k_B T \ln{2} \tau$}
        \State $N_x \gets |(\bar{s}_x^*)^R|$
    \EndWhile
    \State $\tilde{\epsilon}_x^* \gets \tilde{x}_\mu^* - \tilde{x}_b$ \Comment{The normalized phase-space displacement}
    \State \Return $\tilde{\epsilon}_x^*$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}
