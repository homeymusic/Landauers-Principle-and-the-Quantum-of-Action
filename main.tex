\title{Information Erasure Inside the Quantum of Action}

\begin{abstract}
Quantum microstates are operationally indistinguishable inside Planck's quantum of action. We show that among these infinite possible microstates, Landauer's principle compels the selection of the microstate requiring the least erasure action. This erasure granulates the continuum. It resolves the coarse-grained configurations of quantum mechanics at low action into the fine-grained configurations of classical mechanics at high action.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

To understand the mechanics of this model, it is helpful to briefly set aside the traditional, statistical view of the Heisenberg uncertainty principle. Instead, we return to the foundation: Planck's quantum of action, $h$. 

Imagine this fundamental unit not as a statistical blur, but as a physical blob in phase space. The environment can stretch and squeeze this blob into various shapes, but its total area is strictly conserved---it is always exactly $h$. 

Now, suppose the center of this blob sits at an irrational coordinate, such as the golden ratio. In algorithmic information theory, the complexity of a number depends on the reference computer decoding it. If a machine understands symbolic algebra, the golden ratio is a finite string: $(1+\sqrt{5})/2$. However, to pinpoint a physical location in the continuum, we assume the universe's reference machine operates through successive spatial division. It acts as a discrete Szilard engine, narrowing down the coordinate by repeatedly inserting partitions to make a sequence of binary choices. 

On this spatial reference machine, navigating to a rational number like $5/3$ terminates after a finite number of partitions, requiring a short binary program (e.g., \texttt{1101}). But an irrational coordinate like the golden ratio never terminates. To pinpoint it perfectly in the continuum requires an infinitely long sequence of bits. Its algorithmic complexity is infinite.

This is where the math collides with physical reality. Because the universe computes via these physical spatial divisions, the process is inextricably tied to thermodynamics. According to Landauer's principle, processing and erasing information requires a physical expenditure of action---a finite amount of heat dissipated over a finite amount of time for every single bit. To erase an infinitely long binary string would require an infinite amount of physical action. However, any real physical system is bounded by a finite amount of available classical action. The universe simply does not have the budget to handle infinite spatial precision.

Faced with a finite action budget, the universe must truncate. But how is the system allowed to simply swap an exact coordinate for an approximation? Because inside the quantum of action, all microstates are operationally indistinguishable. No physical measurement can tell them apart. This establishes a fundamental many-to-one mapping: an infinite pool of mathematically distinct coordinates within the blob all share the same physical identity. Since any coordinate in this pool is physically valid, the universe selects the microstate that requires the least action to erase.

Instead of the infinitely complex golden ratio, it settles for a nearby simple fraction, like $5/3$. Clearing the excess algorithmic information to reach this low-complexity state is an irreversible thermodynamic process. Erasing this short binary program costs a tiny, manageable amount of physical action that safely fits within the system's classical limits. 

Below, we show that this thermodynamic truncation is the engine of quantum mechanics. Because the universe can only afford finite bit strings, an infinity of precise coordinates is forced to irreversibly erase to a single nearby state. This mandatory Landauer process granulates the continuum. The thermodynamic necessity to expend the least erasure action pixelates phase space, resolving the coarse-grained configurations of quantum mechanics at low action into the fine-grained configurations of classical mechanics at high action.

\section{Landauer: Information Erasure}

Landauer's principle establishes the minimum thermodynamic cost of information erasure \cite{Landauer1961}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LandauerErasureOneBit}
E_L = k_B T \ln{2}
\end{equation}

The total energy dissipated scales with algorithmic information content, defined as the Kolmogorov complexity $K$ \cite{Kolmogorov1965}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LandauerErasureK}
\text{Total Landauer Cost} = K k_B T \ln{2}
\end{equation}


\section{De Gosson: The Quantum Blob}

Phase-space coordinates are $(q,p)$, where $q$ is position and $p$ is momentum. According to the Heisenberg uncertainty principle \cite{Heisenberg1927}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:HeisenbergVariance}
\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}
\end{equation}

Geometrically, symplectic mechanics translates this statistical variance into a $1\sigma$ covariance ellipse in phase space, rigorously bounding the minimum uncertainty region of the state \cite{DeGosson2006}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:CovarianceEllipsoid}
\frac{q^2}{2\sigma_q^2} + \frac{p^2}{2\sigma_p^2} \le 1
\end{equation}

The geometric area $A$ of this ellipse is given by:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:EllipsoidArea}
A = \pi (\sqrt{2}\sigma_q) (\sqrt{2}\sigma_p) = 2\pi \sigma_q \sigma_p
\end{equation}

De Gosson bridges this statistical uncertainty with phase-space geometry. By saturating the Heisenberg bound ($\sigma_q \sigma_p = \hbar/2$), the minimal permissible area emerges as the fundamental quantum blob $A_b$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SimpleLanguageOneBlob}
\frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{Quantum of Action} = \text{Quantum Blob}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SymplecticCapacity}
\frac{h}{2} = A_b
\end{equation}

Protected by Gromov's non-squeezing theorem \cite{Gromov1985}, this $h/2$ minimum area is topologically conserved. Compressing the blob along one axis inevitably forces a proportional expansion along its conjugate.

\section{Margolus-Levitin: Erasure Action}

To constrain Landauer erasure by the classical action, it is dimensionally necessary to introduce a characteristic erasure time, $\tau$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureActionDefinition}
\text{Erasure Action} = K E_L \tau
\end{equation}

We validate this definition at the limit of the minimal quantum phase space, where the pair of quantum blobs degenerate into a perfect overlapping circle with area $h/2$. Saturated by one position bit and one momentum bit, $K = K_q + K_p = 2$, the minimal erasure action becomes:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:MinimalErasureAction}
2 E_L \tau = \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

Solving for $\tau$ recovers the Margolus-Levitin limit \cite{MargolusLevitin1998} for the minimum time to evolve to an orthogonal state ($\tau_\perp$):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:MLErasureTime}
\tau_{K=2} = \frac{h}{4 E_L} = \tau_\perp
\end{equation}

This demonstrates that the Margolus-Levitin minimal orthogonal time corresponds to the maximal erasure time.

\section{Lloyd: Erasure Action and Classical Action}

Lloyd demonstrated that the number of elementary logical operations $N$ a physical system can perform in time $t$ is fundamentally bounded by its total available action, $N \le \frac{2 E t}{\pi \hbar}$ \cite{Lloyd2000}. Applying this limit to the geometry of phase space, the total action required to erase $K$ bits of information cannot exceed the classical action area, $A$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureAndClassicalActionPlain}
\text{Erasure Action} \le \text{Classical Action}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureAndClassicalAction}
K E_L \tau \le A
\end{equation}

\section{Zurek: One Blob or Two?}

Zurek \cite{Zurek2001} demonstrated that a quantum state develops localized structures as the classical action boundaries, $\Delta p$ and $\Delta q$, compress the conjugate squeezed boundaries, $\delta q$ and $\delta p$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:BlobSqueeze}
\delta q = \frac{\hbar}{\Delta p} \quad \text{and} \quad \delta p = \frac{\hbar}{\Delta q}
\end{equation}

Expressing these squeezed boundaries as phase-space ellipses yields a conjugate pair of de Gosson blobs (see Fig.~\ref{fig:erase_schematic}):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:BlobAreas}
\pi \delta q \Delta p = \frac{h}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad \pi \Delta q \delta p = \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

Because both areas are required to bound the space, the total quantum of action $h$ is defined by their sum:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SimpleLanguageBlobs}
\text{Quantum of Action} = \text{Pair of Quantum Blobs}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Blobs}
h = \pi \delta q \Delta p + \pi \Delta q \delta p
\end{equation}

\section{Planck: Quantum Erasure Capacity}

Planck demonstrated \cite{Planck1906} that a classical phase space of area $A$ has a configuration capacity of $A/h$. Expanding the quantum of action $h$ into its pair of quantum blobs (Eq.~\ref{eq:Blobs}), we scale the erasure action (Eq.~\ref{eq:ErasureAndClassicalAction}):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:GeometricActionLimit}
(K_q + K_p) E_L \tau \le \frac{A}{h} (\pi \delta q  \Delta p + \pi \Delta q \delta p)
\end{equation}

Where the erasure paths from the selected microstate $(q_\mu, p_\mu)$ to the blob centers $(q_b, p_b)$ are bounded by $|\epsilon_q| \le \delta q$ and $|\epsilon_p| \le \delta p$.

\subsection{Anatomy of the Erasure Action}

To understand the physical machinery of Equation~\ref{eq:GeometricActionLimit}, we can isolate the provisioning and expenditure of algorithmic information:

\subsubsection{The Quantum Capacity ($A/h$)} 
The area of the conjugate blob pair is the quantum of action, $h$. Because each quantum blob $A_b = h/2$ can only ever fund the minimal bit budget ($K_q = 1, K_p = 1$), the action required to support a classical bit budget ($K \gg 1$) must be provisioned by the classical scale of the system. The multiplier $A/h$ provides this capacity, scaling the quantum of action $h$ up to the classical action necessary to support the total bits in $K$.

\subsubsection{The Geometric Squeezing ($h/2$)} 
When a classical boundary expands (e.g., increasing the classical momentum boundary $\Delta p$), symplectic geometry strictly conserves the area of each de Gosson blob at exactly $h/2$. Because this area cannot grow, the blob must physically compress the squeezed position boundary ($\delta q$) to compensate. 

\subsubsection{The Resolving Power ($K$)} 
This symplectic squeezing acts as the resolving power of the phase space. To pinpoint a microstate within increasingly fine squeezed boundaries ($\delta q \to 0$, $\delta p \to 0$), the universe must expend more algorithmic information ($K_q \to \infty, K_p \to \infty$). Therefore, classical boundaries enforce squeezed resolution, which in turn demands a larger bit budget:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ResolvingPowerPlain}
\substack{\text{Classical} \\ \text{Boundary}}
\sim
\substack{\text{Resolving} \\ \text{Power}} 
\sim
\substack{\text{Bit} \\ \text{Budget}}
\end{equation}

Isolating the conjugate pairs reveals the causal sequence: the expanding classical boundaries drive the inverse scaling of the squeezed boundaries, which dictates the erasure bit budget:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:BitBudgetResolutionCoordinate}
\Delta p \sim \frac{1}{\delta q} \sim K_q 
\quad \text{and} \quad
\Delta q \sim \frac{1}{\delta p} \sim K_p 
\end{equation}

When the classical momentum boundary ($\Delta p$) grows, the squeezed position boundary ($\delta q$) must shrink. The universe must therefore spend more bits ($K_q$) to select and erase the state. This changing bit budget dictates the physics we observe: low action affords only enough bits for discrete quantum configurations, while high action supplies the massive bit budget required to draw smooth classical trajectories.

\section{Quantum Density from Erasure}

Standard phase-space formulations of quantum mechanics traditionally employ the Wigner quasi-probability distribution $W(q,p)$ \cite{Wigner1932}. In the context of information erasure, however, we instead introduce the Landauer erasure density $L(q,p)$ as a sum of discrete Iverson brackets over the $N$ accessible microstates:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:JointErasure}
L(q,p) = \sum_{\mu=1}^{N} \left[ q = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_{q,\mu} \right] \left[ p = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_{p,\mu} \right]
\end{equation}

Because algorithmic erasure evaluates to a strictly discrete coordinate grid, the marginal distributions are obtained by summing over the conjugate variables:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Marginals}
N(q) = \sum_{p} L(q,p)
\quad \text{and} \quad
N(p) = \sum_{q} L(q,p)
\end{equation}

\section{Bennett: Universal Eraser}

Let $U$ be a Universal Reversible Turing Machine. For any phase-space coordinate, $x \in \{q, p\}$, a computable encoding function extracts the selected microstate $x_\mu$ inside the squeezed boundary $\delta x$ centered on the blob coordinate $x_b$, yielding a binary program string $s_x$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:EncoderFunction}
(x_\mu, s_x) = \operatorname{ENCODE}(x_b, \delta x)
\end{equation}

Following Bennett's protocol for logical reversibility \cite{Bennett1973}, we take the bitwise complement $\bar{s}_x$ and reverse the string to form the minimal uncomputation path, with algorithmic complexity $K_x = |s_x^*| = |s_x|$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:KolmogorovComplexity}
s_x^* = (\bar{s}_x)^R
\end{equation}

Algorithmic erasure of this string physically uncomputes the microstate back to the blob center ($x_b$). This process expends Landauer action ($\Delta A_L = K_x E_L \tau$) to the environment, yielding the phase-space displacement $\epsilon_x$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureFunction}
\epsilon_x = \operatorname{ERASE}(s_x^*, x_\mu, x_b), \quad \text{expends} \quad \Delta A_L = K_x E_L \tau
\end{equation}

As each selected microstate $(q_\mu, p_\mu)$ uncomputes to its respective center $(q_b, p_b)$, it generates the conjugate displacements $\epsilon_q$ and $\epsilon_p$. The observed classical coordinates $(q,p)$ are these displacements scaled by Planck's classical capacity $A/h$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ObservedCoordinates}
q = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_q \quad \text{and} \quad p = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_p
\end{equation}

\section{Numerical Simulations}

To simulate the physical encoding function $\operatorname{ENCODE}$, we leverage modified sequences \cite{Aiylam2013} to extend the standard Stern-Brocot tree \cite{ConcreteMath} across the entire real line, $\mathbb{R}$, by initializing the search boundaries at $\pm \infty$:

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{ENCODE}{$x_b, \delta x$}
    \State $\text{left} \gets (-1, 0)$
    \State $\text{num} \gets 0, \quad \text{den} \gets 1$
    \State $x_\mu \gets \text{num} / \text{den}$
    \State $\text{right} \gets (1, 0)$
    \State $s_x \gets \text{``''}$
    \While{$|x_\mu - x_b| > \delta x$}
        \If{$x_\mu < x_b$} 
            \State $\text{left} \gets (\text{num}, \text{den})$
            \State $s_x \gets s_x + \text{``1''}$
        \Else 
            \State $\text{right} \gets (\text{num}, \text{den})$
            \State $s_x \gets s_x + \text{``0''}$
        \EndIf
        \State $\text{num} \gets \text{left}_{\text{num}} + \text{right}_{\text{num}}$
        \State $\text{den} \gets \text{left}_{\text{den}} + \text{right}_{\text{den}}$
        \State $x_\mu \gets \text{num} / \text{den}$
    \EndWhile
    \State \Return $x_\mu, s_x$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}

For example, let $s_x = \texttt{1101}$. Taking the bitwise complement gives $\bar{s}_x = \texttt{0010}$, and reversing it yields the uncomputation path $s_x^* = (\bar{s}_x)^R = \texttt{0100}$.

The minimal program $s_x^*$ is irreversibly destroyed, clearing the tape on $U$. This algorithmic erasure dissipates the Landauer action and yields the observable displacement:

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{ERASE}{$s_x^*, x_\mu, x_b$}
    \State $K_x \gets |s_x^*|$
    \While{$K_x > 0$}
        \State \Call{ShiftLeft}{$s_x^*$} 
        \Statex \Comment{Expends action $k_B T \ln{2} \tau$}
        \State $K_x \gets |s_x^*|$
    \EndWhile
    \State $\epsilon_x \gets x_\mu - x_b$ \Comment{The phase-space displacement}
    \State \Return $\epsilon_x$ \Comment{Classical observation $x = A/h \cdot \epsilon_x$}
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}

\section{Time Evolution and Decoherence}

Uncomputing the selected microstate on $U$ is a physical process that unfolds over time. As the system clears bits from the tape, the microstate steps closer to the classical target, physically shrinking the offsets $\epsilon_q$ and $\epsilon_p$ toward zero. Because the classical coordinates scale directly with these squeezed offsets, they track this continuous erasure process:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:TimeEvolutionCoordinates}
q(t) = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_q(t) \quad \text{and} \quad p(t) = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_p(t)
\end{equation}

Quantum behavior is visible only while algorithmic information remains on the tape. When the tape is fully erased ($\epsilon_q = \epsilon_p = 0$), the coordinates rest exactly at the classical target $(q_b, p_b)$. We observe this mechanical loss of information as quantum decoherence. 

By isolating time from the erasure action and expanding Landauer's limit ($E_L = k_B T \ln 2$), we find the time required for this decoherence:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:DecoherenceTime}
\tau_D = \frac{A}{(K_q + K_p) k_B T \ln 2}
\end{equation}

This denominator sets the physical clock. A system maintains its quantum behavior if it remains small ($K \to 2$) and cold ($T \to 0$). If the state requires a massive bit budget ($K \gg 1$) or operates at a high temperature ($T \gg 0$), the denominator grows rapidly. The erasure time drops to zero, and the system becomes entirely classical.

\section{A Geometric Uncertainty Principle}

Zurek demonstrated that quantum interference of the compass state creates rectangular structures bounded by the squeezed boundaries, with area $a = \delta q \delta p$ \cite{Zurek2001}. Generalizing to symplectic geometry, the unique maximum-volume shape inscribed within this convex body is the Fritz John ellipsoid \cite{John1948}. While de Gosson's formulation \cite{DeGosson2022} saturates at the half quantum of action ($h/2$), erasure within the squeezed boundaries forces this inner geometry to be smaller:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:JohnEllipsoid}
\pi \delta q \delta p \le \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

This establishes a classical action boundary on squeezed uncertainty:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:GeometricUncertaintyPrinciplePlainLanguage}
\text{Uncertainty} \propto \frac{1}{\text{Classical Action}}
\end{equation}

Recalling the squeezed boundaries from Eq.~\ref{eq:BlobSqueeze}, the area of this inscribed ellipse is $\pi \delta q \delta p = \pi(\hbar/\Delta p)(\hbar/\Delta q)$, yielding a geometric uncertainty principle:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ZurekUncertaintyPrinciple}
\delta q \delta p = \frac{\hbar^2}{\Delta q \Delta p}.
\end{equation}

Unlike the traditional uncertainty principle $\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \hbar/2$, which acts as a passive statistical floor, this geometric formulation is operational and resolves Schr{\"{o}}dinger's cat paradox \cite{Schrodinger1980Trimmer}: the vast scale of the classical boundaries actively compresses the squeezed boundaries into fine crosshairs, enforcing squeezed resolution and smoothly transitioning the quantum state into classical reality.

\section{Finite or Infinite Support}

While standard phase-space representations—such as the Wigner distribution and de Gosson's $1\sigma$ quantum blobs—are formally defined with infinite support (e.g., infinitely extending Gaussian tails), the Landauer erasure density proposed here utilizes strict operational boundaries. 

Ultimately, our model remains agnostic to whether the true underlying physical state possesses finite or infinite support. Because the thermodynamic cost of erasure is overwhelmingly dominated by the localized volume of the state, the resulting phase-space physics and action accounting remain consistent in either regime. 

However, from a physical standpoint, modeling these boundaries with compact support arguably offers a conceptual advantage: it avoids the counterintuitive necessity of assigning non-zero probability amplitudes to strictly localized particles at arbitrarily large, classical distances.
