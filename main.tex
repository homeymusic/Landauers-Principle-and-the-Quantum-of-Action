\title{Information Erasure Inside the Quantum of Action}

\begin{abstract}
Quantum microstates are operationally indistinguishable inside Planck's quantum of action. We show that Landauer's principle compels the erasure of the minimal-action microstate, yielding the observed state. This erasure granulates the continuum, smoothly resolving the coarse-grained configurations of quantum mechanics at low action into the fine-grained configurations of classical mechanics at high action.
\end{abstract}

\section{De Gosson: The Quantum Blob}

Phase-space coordinates are $(q,p)$, where $q$ is position and $p$ is momentum. According to the Heisenberg uncertainty principle \cite{Heisenberg1927}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:HeisenbergVariance}
\sigma_q \sigma_p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:CovarianceEllipsoid}
\frac{q^2}{2\sigma_q^2} + \frac{p^2}{2\sigma_p^2} \le 1
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:EllipsoidArea}
A = \pi (\sqrt{2}\sigma_q) (\sqrt{2}\sigma_p) = 2\pi \sigma_q \sigma_p
\end{equation}

Saturating the Heisenberg bound \cite{Heisenberg1927} ($\sigma_q \sigma_p = \hbar/2$) gives the area of the quantum blob $A_b$ \cite{DeGosson2003}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SimpleLanguageOneBlob}
\text{Quantum Blob} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{Quantum of Action}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SymplecticCapacity}
A_b = \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

\section{Zurek: One Blob or Two?}

Zurek \cite{Zurek2001} demonstrated that a quantum state constrained by the classical action boundaries $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ develops sub-Planck structures defined by the reciprocal limits:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:BlobSqueeze}
\delta q = \frac{\hbar}{\Delta p} \quad \text{and} \quad \delta p = \frac{\hbar}{\Delta q}
\end{equation}

Expressing Zurek's reciprocal limits as phase-space areas yields a conjugate pair of de Gosson capacities (see Fig.~\ref{fig:erase_schematic}):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:BlobAreas}
\pi \delta q \Delta p = \frac{h}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad \pi \Delta q \delta p = \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

Because both areas are required to bound the space, the total quantum of action $h$ is defined by their sum:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SimpleLanguageBlobs}
\text{Quantum of Action} = \text{Pair of Quantum Blobs}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Blobs}
h = \pi \delta q \Delta p + \pi \Delta q \delta p
\end{equation}

\section{Landauer: Information Erasure}

Landauer's principle establishes the minimum thermodynamic cost of information erasure \cite{Landauer1961}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LandauerErasureOneBit}
E_L = k_B T \ln{2}
\end{equation}

The total energy dissipated scales with algorithmic information content, defined as the Kolmogorov complexity $K$ \cite{Kolmogorov1965}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LandauerErasureK}
\text{Total Landauer Cost} = K k_B T \ln{2}
\end{equation}

\section{Margolus-Levitin: Erasure Action}

To constrain Landauer erasure to the classical action, it is dimensionally necessary to introduce a characteristic erasure time, $\tau$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureActionDefinition}
\text{Erasure Action} = K E_L \tau
\end{equation}

We validate this definition at the limit of the minimal quantum phase space, where the conjugate ellipses un-squeeze into perfect, overlapping circles, degenerating into a single de Gosson blob of area $h/2$. Saturated by one position bit and one momentum bit ($K = K_q + K_p = 2$), the minimal erasure action becomes:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:MinimalErasureAction}
2 E_L \tau = \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

Solving for $\tau$ recovers the Margolus-Levitin limit \cite{MargolusLevitin1998} for the minimum time to evolve to an orthogonal state ($\tau_\perp$):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:MLErasureTime}
\tau_{K=2} = \frac{h}{4 E_L} = \tau_\perp
\end{equation}

This demonstrates that the Margolus-Levitin minimum for state evolution corresponds to the maximum erasure time, naturally setting the physical timescale for quantum decoherence.

\section{Lloyd: Erasure Action and Classical Action}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureAndClassicalActionPlain}
\text{Erasure Action} \le \text{Classical Action}
\end{equation}

Lloyd demonstrated that the number of elementary logical operations $N$ a physical system can perform in time $t$ is fundamentally bounded by its total available action, $N \le \frac{2 E t}{\pi \hbar}$ \cite{Lloyd2000}. Applying this limit to the geometry of phase space, the total action required to erase $K$ bits of information cannot exceed the classical action capacity, $A$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureAndClassicalAction}
K E_L \tau \le A
\end{equation}

\section{Planck: Quantum Erasure Capacity}

Planck demonstrated \cite{Planck1906} that a classical phase space of area $A$ has a configuration capacity of $A/h$. Expanding the quantum of action $h$ into its pair of quantum blobs (Eq.~\ref{eq:Blobs}), we scale this macroscopic capacity to construct the boundaries of erasure action:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:GeometricActionLimit}
(K_q + K_p) E_L \tau \le \frac{A}{h} (\pi \delta q  \Delta p + \pi \Delta q \delta p)
\end{equation}


Because the erasure paths from the minimal-action microstate $(q_\mu^*, p_\mu^*)$ to the blob centers $(q_b, p_b)$ are bounded by $|\epsilon_q| \le \delta q$ and $|\epsilon_p| \le \delta p$, the erasure action is:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ExactGeometricAction}
(K_q + K_p) E_L \tau \le \frac{A}{h} (\pi |\epsilon_q| \Delta p + \pi \Delta q |\epsilon_p|)
\end{equation}


\subsection{Anatomy of the Erasure Action}

To understand the physical machinery of Equation~\ref{eq:ExactGeometricAction}, we can isolate how the universe provisions and expends algorithmic information:

\subsubsection{The Macroscopic Capacity ($A/h$)} 
The area of the conjugate blob pair is the quantum of action, $h$. Because this minimal area can only ever fund the minimal bit budget ($K_q = 1, K_p = 1$), the action required to support a macroscopic bit budget ($K \gg 1$) must be provisioned by the classical scale of the system. The multiplier $A/h$ provides this capacity, scaling the quantum of action $h$ up to the macroscopic action necessary to support the total bits in $K$.

\subsubsection{The Geometric Squeezing ($h/2$)} 
When a macroscopic classical boundary expands (e.g., increasing momentum $\Delta p$), symplectic geometry strictly conserves the area of the underlying de Gosson blob at exactly $h/2$. Because this area cannot grow, the blob must physically squeeze Zurek's spatial uncertainty ($\delta q$) to compensate. 

\subsubsection{The Resolving Power ($K$)} 
This sub-Planck squeezing acts as the resolving power of the phase space. To pinpoint a microstate within increasingly fine spatial crosshairs ($\delta q \to 0$, $\delta p \to 0$), the universe must expend more algorithmic information ($K_q \to \infty, K_p \to \infty$). Therefore, macroscopic boundaries enforce microscopic resolution, which in turn demands a larger bit budget:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ResolvingPowerPlain}
\substack{\text{Classical} \\ \text{Boundary}}
\sim
\substack{\text{Resolving} \\ \text{Power}} 
\sim
\substack{\text{Bit} \\ \text{Budget}}
\end{equation}

By separating the conjugate coordinates, we see exactly how the erasure bit budget scales inversely with the quantum uncertainty, driven by the classical macroscopic boundaries:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:BitBudgetResolutionCoordinate}
\Delta p \sim \frac{1}{\delta q} \sim K_q 
\quad \text{and} \quad
\Delta q \sim \frac{1}{\delta p} \sim K_p 
\end{equation}

When classical momentum ($\Delta p$) grows, spatial uncertainty ($\delta q$) must shrink. The universe must therefore spend more bits ($K_q$) to locate and erase the state. This changing bit budget dictates the physics we observe: low action affords only enough bits for discrete quantum configurations, while high action supplies the massive bit budget required to draw smooth classical trajectories.

\section{Quantum Density from Erasure}

Instead of the Wigner quasi-probability distribution $W(q,p)$ \cite{Wigner1932}, we introduce the Landauer erasure density $L(q,p)$. While Wigner distributions formally possess infinite support, the thermodynamic cost of erasure is dominated by the localized phase-space volume. Therefore, we treat the classical action boundaries $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ as operational cutoffs, remaining agnostic to the exponentially vanishing tails of the underlying state:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:JointErasure}
L(q,p) = \sum_{\mu=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}\left( \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_q = q \right) \mathbb{I}\left( \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_p = p \right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Marginals}
N(q) = \sum_{p} L(q,p)
\quad \text{and} \quad
N(p) = \sum_{q} L(q,p)
\end{equation}

\section{Bennett: Universal Eraser}

Let $U$ be a Universal Reversible Turing Machine. For any phase-space coordinate, $x \in \{q, p\}$, a computable encoding function, $\mathcal{E}_U$, encodes the minimal-action microstate $x_\mu^*$ inside the classical action boundary $\delta x$ centered on the blob component $x_b$ as a binary program string $s_x$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:EncoderFunction}
\mathcal{E}_U(x_b, \delta x) \rightarrow (x_\mu^*, s_x)
\end{equation}

Following Bennett's protocol for logical reversibility \cite{Bennett1973}, we invert and reverse $s_x$ to form the minimal uncomputation path:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:KolmogorovComplexity}
s_x^* = \operatorname{reverse}(\operatorname{invert}({s_x}))
\end{equation}

Algorithmic erasure of $s_x^*$, where $K_x=|s_x^*|$, incurs Landauer's thermodynamic cost:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ErasureFunction}
\text{ERASE}(s_x^*) \rightarrow K_x E_L \tau
\end{equation}

Each minimal-action microstate $(q_\mu^*, p_\mu^*)$ uncomputes to $(q_b, p_b)$, creating a displacement in phase space $\epsilon_q = q_\mu^* - q_b$ and $\epsilon_p = p_\mu^* - p_b$. The observed macroscopic coordinates $(q,p)$ are those displacements $(\epsilon_q,\epsilon_p)$ scaled by Planck's quantum capacity $A/h$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ObservedCoordinates}
q = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_q \quad \text{and} \quad p = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_p
\end{equation}

\subsection{Time Evolution and Decoherence}

Uncomputing the minimal-action microstate on $U$ is a physical process that unfolds over time. As the system clears bits from the tape, the microstate steps closer to the classical target, physically shrinking the offsets $\epsilon_q$ and $\epsilon_p$ toward zero. Because the macroscopic coordinates scale directly with these microscopic offsets, they track this continuous erasure process:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:TimeEvolutionCoordinates}
q(t) = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_q(t) \quad \text{and} \quad p(t) = \frac{A}{h} \epsilon_p(t)
\end{equation}

Quantum behavior is visible only while algorithmic information remains on the tape. When the tape is fully erased ($\epsilon_q = \epsilon_p = 0$), the coordinates rest exactly at the classical target $(q_b, p_b)$. We observe this mechanical loss of information as quantum decoherence. 

By isolating time from the erasure action boundary and expanding Landauer's limit ($E_L = k_B T \ln 2$), we find the time required for this decoherence:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:DecoherenceTime}
\tau_D = \frac{\text{Erasure Action}}{(K_q + K_p) k_B T \ln 2}
\end{equation}

This denominator sets the physical clock. A system maintains its quantum behavior if it remains small ($K \to 2$) and cold ($T \to 0$). If the state requires a massive bit budget ($K \gg 1$) or operates at a high temperature ($T \gg 0$), the denominator grows rapidly. The erasure time drops to zero, and the system becomes entirely classical.

\section{A Geometric Uncertainty Principle}

Zurek's demonstrated that quantum interference creates rectangular sub-Planck tiles of area $a = \delta q \delta p$ \cite{Zurek2001}. Generalizing these rectangular bounds to symplectic geometry, we bound this uncertainty within an ellipsoid. 

In the context of phase-space polar duality \cite{deGosson2022}, the unique maximum-volume ellipsoid inscribed within a convex body is the Fritz John ellipsoid \cite{John1948}. De Gosson's John ellipsoid is saturated at the half quantum of action ($h/2$), erasure within Zurek's sub-Planck interference forces this inner ellipsoid to be smaller:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:SubPlanckJohnEllipsoid}
\pi \delta q \delta p \le \frac{h}{2}
\end{equation}

The area of this Fritz John ellipsoid establishes a geometric uncertainty relation bounded by the classical action:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:GeometricUncertaintyPrinciplePlainLanguage}
\text{Uncertainty} \propto \frac{1}{\text{Classical Action}}
\end{equation}

The area of the John ellipse is $\pi \delta q \delta p$ giving us a geometric uncertainty principle:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ZurekUncertaintyPrinciple}
\delta q \delta p = \frac{\hbar^2}{\Delta q \Delta p}.
\end{equation}

This approach resolves {S}chr{\"{o}}dinger's cat paradox \cite{Schrodinger1980Trimmer}: macroscopic action squeezes quantum uncertainty into fine crosshairs in phase-space, enforcing microscopic precision and naturally restoring the correspondence principle.

\section{Numerical Simulations}

 To simulate the physical encoding function $\mathcal{E}_U$, we leverage modified sequences \cite{Aiylam2013} to extend the standard Stern-Brocot tree \cite{ConcreteMath} across the entire real line, $\mathbb{R}$, by initializing the search bounds at $\pm \infty$:

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{$\mathcal{E}_U$}{$x_b, \delta x$}
    \State $\text{left} \gets (-1, 0)$
    \State $\text{num} \gets 0, \quad \text{den} \gets 1$
    \State $x_\mu^* \gets \text{num} / \text{den}$
    \State $\text{right} \gets (1, 0)$
    \State $s_x \gets \text{``''}$
    \While{$|x_\mu^* - x_b| > \delta x$}
        \If{$x_\mu^* < x_b$} 
            \State $\text{left} \gets (\text{num}, \text{den})$
            \State $s_x \gets s_x + \text{``1''}$
        \Else 
            \State $\text{right} \gets (\text{num}, \text{den})$
            \State $s_x \gets s_x + \text{``0''}$
        \EndIf
        \State $\text{num} \gets \text{left}_{\text{num}} + \text{right}_{\text{num}}$
        \State $\text{den} \gets \text{left}_{\text{den}} + \text{right}_{\text{den}}$
        \State $x_\mu^* \gets \text{num} / \text{den}$
    \EndWhile
    \State \Return $x_\mu^*, s_x$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}

For example, let $s_x = \texttt{1101}$, then $\operatorname{invert}(\texttt{1101}) = \texttt{0010}$, and $s_x^* = \operatorname{reverse}(\texttt{0010}) = \texttt{0100}$.

The minimal program $s_x^*$ is irreversibly destroyed, clearing the tape on $U$.

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{ERASE}{$s_x^*$}
    \State $K_x \gets |s_x^*|$
    \While{$K_x > 0$}
        \State \Call{ShiftLeft}{$s_x^*$} 
        \Statex \Comment{Dissipate heat $k_B T \ln{2}$ over time $\tau$}
        \State $K_x \gets |s_x^*|$
    \EndWhile
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}
