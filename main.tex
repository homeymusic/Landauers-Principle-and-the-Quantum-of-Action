\title{Information Erasure Inside Quantum Blobs}

\begin{abstract}
Quantum microstates are operationally indistinguishable within finite-support quantum blobs. Within these action areas, the principle of least action compels Landauer information erasure of quantum microstates into minimal-action states. This erasure precludes the classical continuum, necessitating discrete configurations and the emergence of quantum mechanics.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{One Blob or Two?}

In the standard interpretation of the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle, as a system's total action scales up, its minimal phase-space area---the single blob defined by $\Delta q \Delta p$---must expand. However, the foundational architects of quantum mechanics recognized that this mathematical behavior contradicts the macroscopic world. As Schr\"{o}dinger observed in 1926 \cite{Schrodinger1926}, and Einstein formally critiqued in 1953 \cite{Einstein1953}, permitting a single uncertainty cell to grow proportionately with a system's action predicts that everyday objects should exist as delocalized, unobservable smears. 

This unphysical prediction would later drive theorists to formally define the strict boundaries of empirical macrorealism \cite{Popper1967, LeggettGarg1985}. While Schr\"{o}dinger patched this macroscopic paradox by introducing coherent states---relying on the infinite superposition of wavefunctions to force localization---and others relegated the expanding uncertainty strictly to statistical ensembles \cite{Ballentine1970}, we propose a geometric solution. Here, quantum and macroscopic scaling are achieved through a pair of orthogonal, conjugate quantum blob, governed as polar duals $A_{\delta q}$ and $A_{\delta p}$ whose sum strictly conserves the quantum of action $h$.

Where de Gosson \cite{degosson2009iceberg} illustrated the constraint on a single symplectic area by recalling the biblical camel:
\begin{quote}
``\dots It is easier for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle than for one who is rich to enter the kingdom of God\dots''
\end{quote}
\noindent our model requires a conjugate pair. As one symplectic area is squeezed through the needle's eye, its polar dual must proportionately expand---a reciprocal geometry manifested by the \textit{r\'iastrad} (distortion) of the Old Irish hero C\'u Chulainn:
\begin{quote}
``He squeezed one eye no wider than the eye of a needle; he opened the other wider than the mouth of a mead-cup.'' \cite{RiastradNote}
\end{quote}

In the ground state, this conjugate pair is degenerate, operating as a single isotropic circle in phase space. However, as the system's total action increases, the area does not uniformly expand into an unobservable blur. Instead, the geometry squeezes the blobs into a pair of orthogonal ellipses---one stretching horizontally while its polar dual stretches vertically. The orthogonal cigar shapes narrow, ultimately sharpening into fine crosshairs. This precise intersection recovers the sharp, localized point of classical macrorealism, restoring the correspondence principle through convex symplectic geometry.

\section{Numerical Simulations}

\begin{algorithmic}
\Function{erase}{$x_\mu, \delta x$}
    \State $\text{left} \gets (-1, 0), \quad \text{right} \gets (1, 0)$
    \State $\text{num} \gets 0, \quad \text{den} \gets 1$
    \State $x^* \gets \text{num} / \text{den}$
    \State $\mathbf{s}^* \gets \text{""}$
    \While{$|x_\mu - x^*| > \delta_x$}
        \If{$x^* < x_\mu$} 
            \State $\text{left} \gets (\text{num}, \text{den})$
            \State $\mathbf{s}^* \gets \mathbf{s}^* + \text{'1'}$
        \Else 
            \State $\text{right} \gets (\text{num}, \text{den})$
            \State $\mathbf{s}^* \gets \mathbf{s}^* + \text{'0'}$
        \EndIf
        \State $\text{num} \gets \text{left}_{num} + \text{right}_{num}$
        \State $\text{den} \gets \text{left}_{den} + \text{right}_{den}$
        \State $x^* \gets \text{num} / \text{den}$
    \EndWhile
    \State $\epsilon_x \gets x^* - x_\mu$
    \State \Return $x^*, \mathbf{s}^*, \epsilon_x$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}
